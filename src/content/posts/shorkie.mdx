Every cell in your body carries essentially the same DNA, yet a neuron and a skin cell could hardly look more different. The difference is *regulation* — which genes are switched on, how strongly, and exactly when. That program is written into the non-coding DNA around each gene, in a language of transcription-factor binding sites and chromatin signals we still only partly understand. If we could read it fluently — predict a gene's expression directly from its sequence — we could finally interpret the flood of non-coding variants that genetic studies turn up but can't explain.

We're not there yet. Even sophisticated models explain at most around 73% of the variation in gene expression. **Shorkie**, the project I want to tell you about, is our attempt to close some of that gap — and, just as importantly, to do it in a way where we can check whether the model has actually *learned biology* rather than memorized its training set, and whether what it learns is solid enough to read real regulatory variants.

## Why budding yeast?

If you want to test whether a model can learn the rules of gene regulation, budding yeast (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae*) is almost the perfect laboratory. It is the premier model organism for eukaryotic gene regulation: roughly 7,000 genes controlled by hundreds of transcription factors, decades of careful experiments mapping who binds where, and a genome compact enough — about 12 megabases — to model end to end. It is also a cornerstone of aging research: much of what we understand about lifespan, from sirtuins to calorie restriction, was first worked out in yeast. That matters here, because Shorkie was built at Calico, a company devoted to the biology of aging — and learning to read the regulatory code of a premier aging model is squarely in its wheelhouse.

But that compactness cuts both ways. A supervised model learns regulation by example — one gene's sequence paired with its expression, over and over — and roughly 6,000 genes is far too few independent examples to fit a large network without it simply memorizing them. The irony is that yeast is anything but data-*poor*: it has decades of meticulous experiments behind it. What it lacks is enough raw *sequence* for a model to generalize from. It was ideal in every respect except the one deep learning is hungriest for.

## The bet: borrow power from evolution

Shorkie began as my summer-2024 internship in the Kelley Lab at Calico, and our first instinct was the obvious one: take **Borzoi** — the lab's model that predicts RNA-seq coverage directly from DNA sequence ([Linder et al., 2025](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41588-024-02053-6)) — and train it on yeast from scratch. It didn't work. With only ~6,000 genes to learn from, the network overfit — it nailed the genes it had seen and fell apart on the ones it hadn't. The shortage was real, and no amount of architecture tuning was going to conjure more genes out of a 12 Mb genome.

So we changed tack. If yeast's own genome was too small, perhaps its *relatives* could supply what was missing. Across the fungal kingdom, evolution has run essentially the same regulatory experiment millions of times over: the sequences that *matter* for regulation tend to stay conserved, while the rest drifts freely. We were encouraged by a 2024 study from the Gagneur lab showing that DNA language models trained across hundreds of species learn regulatory elements — and even their evolution — far better than alignment to any single genome allows ([Karollus et al., 2024](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13059-024-03221-x)). That was the spark: pretrain a DNA language model on yeast's fungal relatives first, then fine-tune it on yeast itself.

That left us with two questions, not one. The first: could pretraining across many fungal genomes seed the network with conserved, expression-relevant features that supervised learning could never have found from 6,000 genes alone? The second was subtler — *how far across the tree should we reach?* More genomes mean more diversity to learn from, but reach too far and you start importing the regulatory quirks of distant species that have little to do with budding yeast. We suspected there was a sweet spot — an evolutionary scale broad enough to teach the grammar, but not so broad that yeast's signal drowns in noise — and we decided to find it empirically rather than guess.

This is the same self-supervised idea behind large language models, applied to DNA — instead of predicting the next word, the model learns to fill in masked stretches of genome, and in doing so internalizes which patterns are meaningful. And because this was Calico, we could pair it with something most groups don't have: the ability to *generate new data* to fine-tune on. The team produced high-resolution RNA-seq time courses — the Induction Dynamics gene Expression Atlas (IDEA) — using miniaturized chemostats, or "ministats," that let us watch yeast genes respond to a transcription-factor induction minute by minute.

## What Shorkie is

Shorkie is a two-stage model. First comes **pretraining**: we trained a masked DNA language model to reconstruct hidden parts of fungal genomes, and to settle the sweet-spot question we tested four nested training sets of increasing breadth — a single yeast reference genome (R64, ~12 Mb); 80 *S. cerevisiae* strains (~1 Gb); 165 genomes from the broader *Saccharomycetales* order (~2 Gb); and 1,341 genomes spanning the whole fungal kingdom (~42 Gb).

  <strong>Figure 2.</strong> The language model's own report card, by evolutionary scope. Trained on each of the four corpora and scored on held-out <em>S. cerevisiae</em>, the masked LM reconstructs yeast best when pretrained on the 165-genome <em>Saccharomycetales</em> order — lowest validation loss (left) and lowest perplexity (right), ahead of the 1,341-genome kingdom set and the narrower strain and single-genome corpora. The language model itself already prefers the sweet spot.
</ZoomFigure>

The result is the heart of the project, and it isn't what raw scale would predict. Ranked by how much each helped the downstream yeast model, the corpora came out **165-genome order > 1,341-genome kingdom > 80 strains > single genome > no pretraining at all**. So evolutionary diversity genuinely helps — even the kingdom-scale set beat the narrow ones — but the benefit isn't monotonic: the widest net was *not* the best. The 165-genome order was the sweet spot we'd bet on.

Why does the kingdom-scale set fall behind? Not, it turns out, because the model was too small to absorb it — we retrained at four different model sizes and the 165-order corpus stayed on top every time. The culprit is *optimization*. At 42 Gb across 1,341 species, the kingdom corpus is wildly heterogeneous, and because training shuffles all those genomes together, consecutive batches yank the network toward organisms as different as a black truffle and a brewer's yeast. Those competing gradients fight one another; training turns noisy and unstable, and yeast's own signal gets buried. The 165-genome order threads the needle — diverse enough to teach the regulatory grammar, yet close enough to *S. cerevisiae* that the gradients mostly pull the same way and training stays stable.

There was a practical payoff, too. We didn't need to run the full, expensive fine-tuning experiment to rank pretraining sets ahead of time: the better a model was at the pure fill-in-the-blank task on held-out yeast sequence — its *perplexity* — the better it tended to fine-tune. The correlation isn't perfect, but it's a cheap compass for choosing what to pretrain on, and one that should generalize well beyond yeast.

<ZoomFigure src={figEvoDownstream} alt="Two panels: validation Pearson's r learning curves for the four pretraining scopes plus random initialization, and best Pearson's r versus masked-LM perplexity, with the 165-genome Shorkie highest and lowest-perplexity." variant="wide">
  <strong>Figure 3.</strong> …and the gain carries through to Shorkie. After fine-tuning each pretrained model identically on the same yeast data, downstream expression accuracy (validation Pearson's r) lands in the same order — 165-genome order (0.63) &gt; 1,341 kingdom (0.62) &gt; 80 strains (0.57) &gt; single genome (0.55) &gt; no pretraining (0.52) (left). And the tie to the language model is tight: the lower a model's held-out yeast perplexity, the better it fine-tunes (right) — which is exactly why perplexity makes a cheap proxy for choosing pretraining data.
</ZoomFigure>

Then comes **fine-tuning**: we adapted the pretrained model to predict real measurements, unifying 5,215 experimental tracks in a single network — 3,053 high-resolution induction RNA-seq timepoints, about a thousand strain RNA-seq profiles, 1,128 ChIP-exo transcription-factor binding datasets, and 20 histone-modification tracks. The fine-tuning network is a compact, yeast-scale Borzoi: it reads a 16 kb window of DNA, compresses it through convolutions down to a 128 bp transformer core, then reconstructs predictions at 16 bp resolution through a U-Net — all in about 14 million parameters (the language model is 13.7 million; the multi-track output heads add the rest).

Under the hood, the pretraining model has a U-Net shape (Figure 4). During pretraining its only job is self-supervised: predict the identity of every base — A, C, G, or T — across the whole 16,384 bp window. Seven residual down-sampling blocks compress the sequence to a coarse 128 bp representation, where eight Transformer layers integrate long-range context; a mirror-image decoder with skip connections and depthwise-separable convolutions then restores single-base resolution, ending in a softmax over the four nucleotides. It's a small network by modern standards, which is part of the point — most of its power comes from *what* it was trained on, not from raw scale.

  <strong>Figure 4.</strong> The Shorkie LM architecture. The 13.7-million-parameter model reads a 16,384 bp window and predicts per-base nucleotide probabilities: a 1D convolution plus seven residual down-sampling blocks reduce the sequence to a 128 × 384 representation, eight Transformer layers integrate long-range context, and a U-Net decoder with skip connections restores it to full length before a final softmax over A/C/G/T.

## What it showed

Three findings stand out to me.

**Pretraining transfers, measurably.** Fine-tuned from the fungal language model, Shorkie predicts gene-level expression with a Pearson correlation of 0.88, versus 0.74 for an identical model trained from scratch — essentially the supervised approach we had started with — and it wins on 87.8% of genes. That 0.74 → 0.88 jump is the whole pivot in a single number: the grammar learned across the fungal tree really does carry back to yeast. And it isn't an artifact of a lazy comparison. We tuned the from-scratch baseline hard — sweeping its learning rate across three orders of magnitude, even shrinking the network in case it was simply over-parameterized for so little data — and the gap held every time. Pretraining, not tuning, is what closed it.

**It learned biology, not the answer key.** This is the part I most wanted to verify. Using *in-silico* mutagenesis — asking the model how its prediction shifts when we perturb each base — Shorkie rediscovered canonical transcription-factor motifs *without ever being told they exist*: Reb1, Tye7, Cbf1, the poly(dA:dT) tracts that position nucleosomes, the TATA box, Rap1 sites in ribosomal-protein promoters, and the PAC motif of ribosome biogenesis, with Abf1 sites besides. It even picked up 5′ splice-donor and branch-point signals. You can't recover that grammar by memorizing expression values; the model had to internalize the underlying logic.

It's worth being precise about *what* we're reading out, because we used two different lenses and they don't measure the same thing. One is the pretrained language model's own sense of what's *conserved* — the sequence it finds most predictable across the fungal genomes. The other is in-silico mutagenesis on the fine-tuned model, which marks what's *causal* for the expression it predicts: the bases that actually move the output when you change them. Conservation and causal effect needn't coincide — a deeply conserved motif might be silent in a given condition, and a base with real causal pull might not be especially conserved. So when both lenses land on the *same* sites, that convergence is the strongest evidence the model has found something real. You can see it in Figure 5: at the *RPL26A* locus, both readouts pick out the Fhl1 and Rap1 binding sites and the 5′ splice donor, lining up with the reference annotation — while an identical model *without* pretraining (the `Shorkie_Random_Init` control) stays essentially flat.

**It's useful for variants — and honest about where.** A model that reads regulatory grammar ought to be able to call the effect of a regulatory *variant*, so we tested Shorkie on *cis*-eQTLs — the non-coding changes that nudge a gene's expression up or down. Across three independent yeast eQTL datasets, it beat the specialized DREAM-challenge models at separating real eQTLs from matched controls. It also beat both its own from-scratch twin *and* the pretrained language model used on its own, with no fine-tuning. That second comparison is the one I find most telling: conservation alone — everything the language model knows — isn't enough; fine-tuning on real measurements adds genuine causal signal on top of it.

But "beats DREAM" deserves an asterisk, and the asterisk is the interesting part. The DREAM models were trained on *synthetic* DNA — millions of designed promoter variants measured in isolation by massively parallel reporter assays (MPRAs). On that home turf, predicting effects inside synthetic MPRA constructs, the MPRA-trained model beats Shorkie, even though Shorkie had never seen an MPRA in training. Each model is simply strongest in the domain it was raised on: Shorkie on native genomic sequence, the DREAM model on designed constructs.

That tension sits at the center of a real debate in the field — what de Boer and Taipale have called *holding out the genome*. The catch with learning from natural sequence is that the genome is riddled with *correlations* that aren't causation: highly expressed genes happen to share all sorts of incidental sequence features, so a model can look accurate by leaning on associations that would collapse under a controlled experiment. MPRAs were built precisely to break those correlations apart. So the sharpest test of whether Shorkie learned *cause* rather than *correlation* is to point it at variants that have been functionally validated as causal — and when we did, scoring MPRA-validated causal variants in their native promoter context, Shorkie came out ahead of the DREAM models and of the conservation-only language model alike. On the variants we can be most confident are genuinely causal, the model trained on whole genomes wins.

Finally, because Shorkie was trained on time courses, it captures regulation as a *moving target*. As we induced the stress-response factors Msn2/Msn4, the model's reliance on their STRE binding motif sharpened over the first 90 minutes, tracking the real biology of activation — and this isn't a cherry-picked locus. Genome-wide, predicted and measured fold-changes after induction agreed with per-regulator correlations of roughly 0.5–0.65, across twelve timepoints and some 218,000 gene–regulator pairs.

## The bigger picture

For me, Shorkie is a small, clean demonstration of a principle I increasingly believe runs through computational biology: when labeled data is scarce, the way forward is to borrow statistical strength from everywhere else — across species, across assays, across time. A model trained over many genomes learns a grammar that no single genome can teach it, and self-supervised pretraining plus transfer learning beats training any one task in isolation. What decides how much pretraining buys you, I think, is the ratio between two quantities: how little supervised signal your organism affords, against how much related sequence you can borrow. Small-genome systems like yeast sit at the extreme of that ratio, which is exactly why they gain the most — and why yeast is the ideal place to prove the point, small enough to interrogate exhaustively and well-understood enough that we know when the model is right.

It's an idea that travels, too. Another group has already built on Shorkie — a model they call ExoShorkie — transferring its pretrained weights to predict how yeast reads *exogenous* DNA, from human and bacterial to fully synthetic sequences, and finding independently that starting from Shorkie beats starting from scratch. The recipe outlives the specific model.

There's plenty left to do. Shorkie is largely blind to 3′ splice-acceptor mutations; its resolution is coarser than single-base; and it predicts abundant signals like RNA-seq far better than sparse ones like transcription-factor binding. There's a deeper caveat too, the one the eQTL story hinted at: however accurate Shorkie is on native genes, some of that accuracy may still ride on genomic *correlation* rather than pure cause, and teasing the two apart — with more functionally validated variants — is the honest next step. And the real prize — carrying this recipe to the human genome, where the regulatory code reaches across far longer distances and the variants matter far more — is still ahead. But the throughline is clear, and it's why I'm drawn to this kind of work: a model that reads DNA and predicts function lets us interrogate the regulatory code directly, one nucleotide at a time.

Shorkie is open source — the code and trained models are on GitHub.
