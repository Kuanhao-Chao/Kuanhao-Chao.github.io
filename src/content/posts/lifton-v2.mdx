When we built [LiftOn](/posts/lifton/), the pitch was simple: DNA alignment and protein alignment fail in different ways, so don't choose between them — combine them, and let the protein decide what a correct gene ought to look like. That was the idea, and it worked. But an idea that works on one chromosome isn't the same thing as a tool you can point at a genome and walk away from. The time since has gone into the quieter, less glamorous half of the job: making LiftOn hold up at the scale people actually want to use it.

That scale has moved. Complete, telomere-to-telomere assemblies and whole pangenomes are now routine, and people don't run lift-over on a tidy test chromosome — they run it on entire RefSeq genomes, dozens of species at a time. Pushed that hard, the first version showed its seams. It could crash partway through a full plant genome and hand you a half-finished annotation. A single giant gene like *titin* could drive it past forty gigabytes of memory. It carried protein-coding genes but silently left pseudogenes, long non-coding RNAs, and the rest of the non-coding annotation behind. And it ran on a single core while your genome sat there waiting. A method that works on a chromosome but falls over on the genome isn't finished.

**LiftOn 2.0** is that unfinished half — the same protein-maximization idea, rebuilt to run whole genomes quickly, lightly, completely, and without falling over. And because changing the internals of a tool people depend on safely meant measuring everything, it comes with the most thorough benchmark of LiftOn we have ever done.

## What changed under the hood

The first thing I built wasn't a feature — it was a guarantee. Reworking the guts of a tool is dangerous precisely because it's so easy to make it faster and subtly wrong at the same time. So before touching anything, I pinned the output: a test matrix runs the pipeline under every combination of its fast-path options — streaming, in-memory handoff, one to four threads, in-process alignment — and demands the result be *byte-for-byte identical* to the plain default run. With that net in place I could be ruthless about the internals, because anything that changed a single byte of output failed loudly and immediately.

The worst problem was memory. LiftOn aligns every lifted protein back to the reference to score it, and an exact alignment is quadratic in length — fine for an ordinary gene, catastrophic for a giant like *titin*, whose single alignment could swallow tens of gigabytes and take the whole run down with it. LiftOn 2.0 aligns in anchored windows instead: it finds confident matches, chains them in order, and aligns only the gaps between. This is exact wherever the two proteins genuinely line up — the common case — and bounded everywhere else. On one mouse run, peak memory for the giant genes fell from roughly 45 GB to 2.6 GB.

Then speed, from several directions at once. The two aligners LiftOn depends on, Liftoff and miniprot, now run *concurrently* instead of one after the other. The per-gene work is spread across threads. miniprot is finally told how many cores it may use, instead of quietly defaulting to four. And a cluster of database queries that fired two or three times per gene were collapsed into one. None of this changes the answer — the byte-identity matrix saw to that — but together they make the same run noticeably faster and far lighter.

LiftOn 2.0 also lifts more of the genome. The original carried protein-coding genes and stopped; everything else was left behind. The new default detects every *gene-like* feature type in the reference — pseudogenes, non-coding RNA genes, structured mobile elements — and lifts them all. The merge itself got more careful, too: where the old version always applied its combined gene model, 2.0 keeps it only when it actually scores higher against the reference protein than the plain DNA lift, so the merge can only ever help. And the crashes — running across whole genomes turned up two, one I inherited and one I introduced making the completeness change above — are both fixed. Each was the kind of bug you only ever hit at scale, which is exactly why scale was the point. Every change that alters the output keeps an opt-out flag, so the precise numbers from the paper remain reproducible.

## What we measured

To make changes like these with any confidence, I had to know exactly what each one did to the output. So I built a benchmark harness and pointed it at twenty datasets arranged along a *divergence ladder* — from same-species lift-overs (human, mouse, rice, *Arabidopsis*, honeybee, between independent assemblies) out to increasingly distant cross-species pairs (mouse to rat, chicken to quail, *C. elegans* to *C. briggsae*, human to mouse) — across two annotation databases, RefSeq and Ensembl. On every dataset I ran four tools side by side: **Liftoff** (DNA alone), **miniprot** (protein alone), the previous **LiftOn v1.0.8**, and **LiftOn 2.0**. And every tool's output is scored the same neutral way — translate each lifted gene's coding sequence, align it back to the reference protein, measure identity — so no tool gets to grade its own homework.

**It's the most accurate tool in the field — and the lead grows with distance.** Across all twenty datasets, LiftOn 2.0 matches or beats the better of the two single-method baselines on 19, and matches or beats the old version on 19. On same-species lift-overs everyone clusters near perfect, as they should. The interesting part is what happens as the species diverge (Figure 1A): Liftoff, working from DNA alone, falls off a cliff; miniprot, reading the more conserved protein, holds up better; and LiftOn, free to use both, stays highest and pulls *further* ahead exactly where the problem is hardest — mouse to rat (+0.056 over the best single method), chicken to quail (+0.050), *C. elegans* to *C. briggsae* (+0.072). The one place a single tool wins is human to mouse, a brutal mammalian jump where miniprot's protein alignment edges LiftOn out by under a hundredth — an honest reminder that at extreme distance the protein is the signal to trust. And on identical inputs (Figure 1C), the more careful merge improves many genes and breaks almost none: on fruit fly it improved 115 transcripts and regressed exactly one.

**It finishes genomes the old version couldn't.** The single most important result isn't an accuracy number at all — it's that LiftOn 2.0 *finishes*. Pointed at the full *Arabidopsis* RefSeq genome, the old version crashed partway through and finished barely a quarter of the annotation; on rice it stalled at about three-quarters. LiftOn 2.0 runs both to completion (Figure 2A), which by itself recovers 34,589 more coding transcripts on *Arabidopsis* and 9,594 more on rice. A tool that dies a quarter of the way through a genome isn't slightly worse than one that finishes — it's unusable for that genome, and closing that gap is the whole point of this release.

**It lifts the parts of the genome the old version ignored.** Because 2.0 lifts every gene-like feature type by default, the annotations it produces are simply more complete (Figure 2B). On *Arabidopsis* it carries 4,816 pseudogenes the old version didn't emit at all, and substantially more of the rest of the non-coding annotation — thousands more long non-coding RNA genes, and hundreds more transfer RNAs and small nucleolar RNAs. These aren't protein-coding genes, but they are real annotation, and leaving them behind quietly truncates whatever you build next. One stubborn case remains honest about its limits: mature microRNAs, which sit nested inside another feature in a way LiftOn still doesn't handle well, come through at a dismal 1 in 428. It's on the list.

**It's faster and far lighter.** On identical inputs, LiftOn 2.0 is faster end to end — modestly on small genomes, more than twice as fast on others (honeybee 2.15×, mouse-to-rat 2.03×) — and the memory difference is dramatic: the windowed aligner cuts peak RAM by up to 23.9× on the very same runs (Figure 3). That's the difference between a job that needs a dedicated compute node and one that runs on a laptop. And it stays correct while doing it — judged by an independent GFF3 validator, 2.0's output is as clean as the old version's or cleaner on nearly every dataset, and markedly cleaner on the full genomes.

## The bigger picture

When I wrote about the first LiftOn, I called it plumbing — not a flashy model, but the layer everything downstream stands on. Two years of watching people use it taught me that good plumbing isn't only about being *right*; it's about being *dependable* — finishing the job, fitting in the memory you actually have, not quietly dropping a quarter of the annotation. Most of LiftOn 2.0 is that kind of unglamorous work, and I've come to think it matters at least as much as the idea it serves. The most useful thing I did this round wasn't an algorithm; it was the byte-identity net that let me rebuild the engine without changing the answer.

The other lesson is about restraint. A good part of this work was chasing *more* accuracy — smarter ways to pick reading frames, to rescue marginal genes, to fold in still more of the protein evidence. I built and measured each one, and most of them didn't pay: the gains were lost in the noise, or came with regressions that outweighed them. Reporting those honestly and *not* shipping them is as much a part of the work as the wins. A tool that only ever grows is a tool quietly accumulating risk; knowing when an idea is good enough to keep out is its own kind of progress.

LiftOn 2.0 is free and open source, and it still stands on Liftoff and miniprot — two tools each doing what it does best, with LiftOn refereeing between them. If you have a freshly assembled genome and an annotation you trust, point 2.0 at it: it will carry more of the map across, faster and on a fraction of the memory than before — and it will finish.
