Almost every RNA-seq analysis begins the same way: you align millions of short reads back to the genome. Wherever a read jumps across an intron, the aligner records a *splice junction* — evidence of where the cell cut the pre-mRNA and joined the exons. The trouble is that alignment-derived junctions are not all equally trustworthy. Alignment errors, sequencing artifacts, and noisy transcription or splicing can produce low-confidence junctions that inflate false introns and distort transcript assembly. If you want to trust a transcriptome, you first need a way to separate well-supported splice junctions from noise.

**Splam** is the algorithm I built to do that.

## Why not just use SpliceAI?

By the time I started, [SpliceAI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2018.12.015) had already shown that a deep neural network could recognize splice sites directly from sequence. It was the state of the art for splice-site prediction, but it was built for a different job than ours, and the mismatch mattered in three ways.

First, context. SpliceAI uses a 10 kb flanking context, and in our junction-level benchmark it was given the full intron plus flanking sequence. That is far more sequence than the local splice signals, branch point, and nearby regulatory motifs that the splicing machinery is expected to read. Second, scope. SpliceAI was trained on one canonical protein-coding isoform per gene, while many human genes have multiple annotated isoforms. For a tool that evaluates RNA-seq junctions, alternative splice junctions are not an edge case; they are part of the target. Third, framing. SpliceAI predicts donor and acceptor sites as positions in sequence, whereas a splice junction is defined by a *pair*: a donor and an acceptor that belong to the same intron.

In the Salzberg and Pertea labs, where I did my Ph.D., transcript assembly is daily bread, and the practical question we kept hitting was simpler than "where are all the splice sites?" — it was "which of these aligned junctions can I trust?" So I set out to build a compact splice-junction recognition algorithm, focused on the junction as the unit, that we could actually drop into an RNA-seq pipeline. It was my first splice-site deep-learning project — its ideas later grew into [OpenSpliceAI](/posts/openspliceai/).

## The algorithm

Splam starts with a candidate intron: a proposed donor site and a proposed acceptor site, usually coming from an annotation or an RNA-seq alignment. It then builds a fixed-length representation of that candidate using exactly **800 nucleotides**: a 400 nt window centered on the donor site and a 400 nt window centered on the acceptor site. A deep dilated residual convolutional network reads that paired input and outputs a per-base probability for donor, acceptor, or neither, with the junction score derived from the two candidate positions.

</ZoomFigure>

Two choices set the algorithm apart from SpliceAI. It uses a short, biologically motivated window because the paper's design assumes that the decisive splice-site information is concentrated near the donor, acceptor, branch point, and nearby regulatory motifs. And it scores the donor and acceptor *together*, as a junction, mirroring the paired nature of an intron. This design is meant for evaluating putative splice junctions from annotation or RNA-seq alignments; it is not an exhaustive chromosome-wide search over every possible donor-acceptor pair. We also trained the neural predictor on splice junctions drawn from multiple isoforms in annotated protein-coding genes, so alternative junctions were present during training rather than treated as mistakes.

## What it showed

**The advantage shows up most clearly on alternative junctions.** On held-out human junctions, Splam reached 96.1% junction-level accuracy at a score threshold of 0.8 on the Test40K benchmark. On MANE splice sites, Splam and SpliceAI were nearly identical. The separation appeared on alternative splice junctions from RefSeq but not MANE, where Splam produced fewer false negatives despite using much less sequence. In the paper's threshold-0.1 disagreement analysis, Splam correctly recovered **2,609** true splice junctions that SpliceAI missed, while SpliceAI recovered **202** that Splam missed.

<ZoomFigure src={figVsSpliceAI} alt="A chart showing Splam recovers thousands of splice junctions SpliceAI misses — 2,609 at the marked threshold — while missing only 202 that SpliceAI catches." variant="wide">
  <strong>Figure 2.</strong> Catching alternative junctions. Across score thresholds, Splam recovers more true splice junctions that SpliceAI labels negative (green) than the reverse case (orange). At the 0.1 threshold highlighted in the paper, the counts are 2,609 versus 202.
</ZoomFigure>

There is a tradeoff worth naming. In that same comparison, Splam had more false positives than SpliceAI at the selected threshold: 145 versus 16. The paper argues that, for this use case, false negatives on annotated MANE or RefSeq junctions are more likely to be genuine errors than false positives among splice-site-like unannotated sequences, which may include low-level or incompletely annotated splicing. That is the reasoning behind prioritizing recovery of true junctions while still using score thresholds to filter low-confidence alignments.

Because Splam scores a junction as a pair, its donor and acceptor scores also stay more consistent with each other. The algorithm generalized without retraining in the paper's tests on chimpanzee, mouse, and *Arabidopsis thaliana*: at a threshold of 0.8, junction recall was 91%, 87%, and 80%, compared with 57%, 47%, and 20% for SpliceAI on the same species.

**And it cleans up alignments.** This is where the name comes from, and the reason I built it. In the workflow evaluated in the paper, Splam sits after spliced alignment and before transcript assembly: it pulls each alignment's splice junctions out of the BAM file, scores them, and removes alignments containing spurious low-scoring junctions — handing the assembler a cleaner file.

<ZoomFigure src={figCleanup} alt="The Splam workflow: inserted after read alignment in an RNA-seq pipeline, it extracts and scores splice junctions and removes spurious spliced alignments before transcript assembly." variant="wide">
  <strong>Figure 3.</strong> Splam as a pipeline step. <strong>(a)</strong> After reads are aligned (HISAT2/STAR), Splam cleans the spurious spliced alignments before transcript assembly (StringTie). <strong>(b)</strong> Internally, Splam extracts each alignment's junctions, scores them as good or bad, and writes out a cleaned BAM alongside a separate file of the removed alignments.
</ZoomFigure>

The payoff in the paper is concrete and bounded. In 20 human RNA-seq samples aligned with HISAT2, Splam cleaning raised intron precision while keeping RefSeq intron recall nearly unchanged: +14 to +25 percentage points of intron precision in 10 poly(A)-capture samples with less than 1% recall loss, and +28 to +37 points in 10 rRNA-depletion samples with a 0.7 to 1.0% recall drop. When the cleaned BAMs were assembled with StringTie, transcript precision improved in both library types; in the rRNA-depletion samples, the assemblies also gained about 500 to 600 RefSeq-matching transcripts.

## The bigger picture

Splam is a focused algorithm with a clear job: evaluate candidate splice junctions and use those scores to improve RNA-seq alignments. The lessons I carried forward are the design choices that survived the benchmarks. Local sequence around splice sites can be enough for this candidate-junction problem. Modeling the biological unit — here, the donor-acceptor pair — can make the two ends of an intron behave more consistently. And training on multiple annotated isoforms matters when the algorithm will see alternative junctions in real RNA-seq data. Those same instincts shaped my later splicing work, including [OpenSpliceAI](/posts/openspliceai/), the open, retrainable reimplementation of SpliceAI.

Splam is free and open source, with code and documentation online for applying the same junction-scoring and alignment-cleaning workflow.
